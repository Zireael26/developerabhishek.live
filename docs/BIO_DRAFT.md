## 1. Hero tagline — three candidates

The hero tagline is the first thing a prospect reads. It must (a) say what you do for a business, (b) be legible to a textile trader who has never heard "agentic AI," and (c) hold up under a senior engineer's eye. Three angles below — pick one, or use a phrase from one and a beat from another.

### Option A — Outcome-first, MSME-readable

> **AI systems for businesses that haven't met AI yet.**
> I design and build the agents and back-end systems that quietly do the work — for distributors, founders, and teams who'd rather see results than slide decks.

Why this lands: it names the audience without naming "MSME," it's plain English, and "quietly do the work" pre-empts the AI-hype reflex. Why it might not: "haven't met AI yet" is poetic and a senior buyer might want sharper.

### Option B — Craft-first, dual-audience

> **I build agent systems that go to production — and stay there.**
> Six years of shipping AI and platform work, most recently the agents framework behind Bluehost's AI products.

Why this lands: signals seniority and operational chops in one sentence; the second line gives the technical buyer the proof point fast. Why it might not: it leads with engineering language that an MSME owner may not parse on first read.

### Option C — Identity-first, plain

> **Abhishek Kaushik. AI engineer. I build the systems your business will actually use.**
> Agents, RAG, back-end platforms — built end-to-end and shipped to real users. Currently doing this for Bluehost; previously for institutional knowledge systems and a small set of independent products.

Why this lands: name-first is honest; "actually use" is the right kind of pointed. Why it might not: it's three short sentences instead of one — some readers want a single line.

**Recommendation:** Option A on the home hero, with Option B's second line surfaced as a sub-line on hover or as a one-liner in the About section. That gives MSME readers the warm entry and technical readers the proof beat without forcing either to wait.

> I'm Abhishek — an AI engineer who builds agent systems that businesses can actually run. For the last six years I've been working on AI and platform engineering, most recently on the agents framework backend that powers Bluehost's AI products. Outside of that, I'm building Neev, a modular operations platform for Indian MSMEs starting with textile distribution — because the most exciting place for AI right now isn't another consumer chatbot, it's the 63 million businesses still running on WhatsApp messages and paper ledgers. ## 4. Anti-patterns to avoid (style notes)

- **Avoid "passionate."** It's the most-used and least-evidenced word on engineering portfolios. The work is the evidence.
- **Avoid "leveraging."** Use "use." Always.
- **Avoid "cutting-edge."** Either name the specific edge or don't claim it.
- **Avoid stacking adjectives.** "Scalable, robust, performant" reads as filler. Pick one and earn it.
- **Avoid the word "passionate" again.** Worth repeating.
- **Avoid "guru / ninja / wizard / rockstar."** Already obvious, listed for completeness.
- **Don't bury the MSME thesis.** It's the differentiator. It belongs near the top of every long-form bio.
- **Don't over-claim Bluehost ownership.** Honest framing about scope (you have a major hand in maintaining and improving the platform; you don't own it solo) reads stronger than vague big-claims.
